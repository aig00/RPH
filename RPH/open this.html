Moonlit Beginnings: The Story of Bulan

The origins of Bulan trace back to the pre-Hispanic era. The name "Bulan" is believed to derive from the local word for "moon." According to legend, Spanish explorers, upon seeing a radiant moon while sailing near the coast, inquired about it, and the natives responded with "Bulan," leading to the town's name.

Between 1799 and 1801, Bulan was reestablished as a town, becoming a refuge for individuals from Panay and Samar islands during the abaca trade era.

Significant historical landmarks include the Immaculate Conception Parish Church, constructed in 1866, serving as a spiritual center since the 1600s. The Old Municipal Building now houses the Bulan Heritage Museum, preserving artifacts that narrate the town's rich past. Additionally, a World War II-era tunnel in Barangay San Francisco offers glimpses into Bulan's wartime history.

Padaraw Festival and Traditions

The Padaraw Festival, celebrated annually on May 30, is a vibrant expression of Bulan's cultural heritage. The term "Padaraw" is derived from an indigenous Bicol word referring to a school of fish, symbolizing unity. The festival honors the town's patron saint, Our Lady of the Immaculate Conception, and serves as a thanksgiving for the bounties from the sea, plains, and mountains.

Festivities include colorful street dances, parades, and communal gatherings, reflecting the town's gratitude and communal spirit.

Tradition

  • Padaraw Festival: Celebrated annually on May 30, this festival highlights unity and gratitude, symbolized by "daraw," a school of fish. Events include street dancing, parades, and thanksgiving activities.
  • Pista sa Gadan (All Souls' Day): Observed on November 1 and 2, families visit cemeteries to honor the dead with prayers, candles, and flowers, demonstrating deep familial reverence.
